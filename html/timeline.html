Programmed track-finding firmware for an ATLAS detector upgrade
— Jan 2018 – June 2020 —
Graduate Researcher
UIUC, CERN
CERN processes a high volume of streaming data from particle collisions (about a petabyte per second). In order to successfully deal with this data, you need several layers of reconstruction algorithms working across both hardware and software.

For this detector upgrade, we were increasing our luminosity even more, and required a new firware-level processing step. I developed FPGA algorithms to partially reconstruct ~100k collision events per second. My part of the firmware was in charge of segmenting and storing detections, flushing old detections, and performing simple pattern matching at nanosecond time scales.
Created ML algorithms to identify particles from calorimeter decay showers, and generate simulated showers
— Feb 2017 – Nov 2019 —
Graduate Researcher
UIUC, CERN
This work was for CLIC, a proposed linear collider. We were interested in seeing how particle reconstruction and identification could be improved using CLIC's new, more-granular, calorimeter geometry.

I lead a team of nine researchers for this project, and presented results at international workshops and seminars, including NeurIPS.

The work involved training vision recognition algorithms on point-cloud data from simulated decay showers in the CLIC calorimeters. We created algorithms that could determine particle decay type, estimate particle energy, and reconstruct flight trajectory. We improved classification accuracies over existing techniques by over 40x in certain energy regimes useful for next-gen physics searches.

Furthermore, we showed that the tool could be adapted for use in existing detectors with non-uniform calorimeter geometries, like ATLAS and CMS, still outperforming existing reconstruction algorithms.

We also created another GAN-based tool that could generate simulated calorimeter showers. This was to decrease computational load for event simulation, which took up most of CERN's computing resources.

This training was performed on the Blue Waters supercomputing cluster at UIUC.
Improved algorithm to reconstruct vertices in particle collision events
— Apr 2015 – Apr 2016 —
Graduate Researcher
UIUC, CERN
With an LHC luminosity upgrade coming up, CERN needed to improve most of its reconstruction algorithms to deal with the larger volume of data. In a typical event, where two bunches of protons cross with each other, multiple collisions can occur. This problem would be greatly exacerbated by the higher luminosity, requiring better algorithms to reconstruct the separate collision vertices.

I applied unsupervised clustering and FFT-based techniques on 3D voxelated data to improve our vertex finding, improving reconstruction efficiency and computing time over the previous best algorithm. I presented this work at several international conferences.

Measured plasmon propagation physics on metamaterials
— Jan 2012 – July 2013 —
Undergrad Researcher
UT Austin
Our lab was interested in creating and measuring the properties of metamaterials, which were materials grown in such a way that they had interesting optical properties. My project involved epitaxially grown silver, where there were very few grain boundaries in the atomic structure.

In this work, I set up and operated a laser system for launching plasmonic resonances on a target material. We tested plasmon propagation distances on epitaxial silver and thermally-deposited silver, showing longer propagation in the epitaxial material in accordance with theory.

For this project I worked with thermal vapor deposition, characterized materials with ellipsometry, set up femtosecond optics systems, worked with cryogenics, etched grooves with electron beam microscopy, and used AFM microscopy.

For more information you can read my undergrad thesis.
